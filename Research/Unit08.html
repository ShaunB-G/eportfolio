Charts Worksheet and Analysis

Learning Outcomes:

  • Understand how the concepts of validity, generalisability and reliability affect your investigation and the design of your research method.
  • Consider how to analyse and present the results you obtain from your investigation and how they will enable you to answer your research question.

    • The activity involved using Excel workbooks to Analyse a variety of data in order to create graphs.

    Bar Charts in Excel

    Exercise 8.1

    Diet A Diet B
    n 50 50
    Mean 5.341 3.710
    SD 2.536 2.783

    Interpretation

    Diet A appears to produce higher average results with less variability among participants. This means that not only does Diet A lead to better average outcomes, but the outcomes are also more consistent. Diet B produces lower average results and has greater variability among participants. This means that while some participants may achieve high results, others may have significantly lower results, leading to a wider spread of outcomes. In conclusion, Diet A seems to be more effective overall, providing higher and more consistent results compared to Diet B.

    Exercise 8.2

    Frequencies and Percentages
    Area 1 Frequency Area 1 Percentage Area 2 Frequency Area 2 Percentage
    A 11 15.7% 19 21.1%
    B 17 24.3% 30 33.3%
    Other 42 60% 41 45.6%
    Total 70 100% 90 100%

    Interpretation

    Diet A appears to be more effective if the higher values of the measured variable are considered desirable (e.g., greater weight loss, higher improvement in health markers). Diet B has more variability and a lower average performance compared to Diet A, but it also indicates a wider range of outcomes within the middle 50% of the data. In conclusion, Diet A tends to produce higher and more consistent results, while Diet B has greater variability with some participants possibly achieving lower outcomes.

    Exercise 8.3

    Class Relative Frequency
    -3 0.02
    -1 0.04
    1 0.20
    3 0.30
    5 0.22
    7 0.16
    9 0.04
    11 0.02

    Interpretation

    In the frequency table, the relative frequencies indicate the proportion of the sample data within each class interval. For example, 30% of the data falls within the class interval centered around 3, while only 2% of the data falls within the class intervals centered around -3 and 11. The distribution of data seems to peak at 3, suggesting that most of the values are around this interval, with fewer values in the extremes.
